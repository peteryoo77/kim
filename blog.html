Thirty Years of Watching Government — What I've Actually Learned

I started studying public administration in the early 1990s, when Korea was still finding its footing as a democracy. The authoritarian era had just ended. There was a palpable hunger — in classrooms, in policy circles, in the streets — for institutions that could be trusted. Everyone agreed that government needed to change. The disagreements were about how, and how fast, and who would bear the cost of getting it wrong.

Thirty years later, I am still asking the same questions. The vocabulary has changed. We talk now about digital transformation, evidence-based policy, governance ecosystems, citizen-centered design. The frameworks multiply. The conferences fill up. And yet the gap between what government promises and what people experience remains stubbornly wide in ways that no new terminology seems to close.

So what have I actually learned? A few things — not grand theories, just observations that have held up across different administrations, different reform cycles, different decades.

Trust is not built by communication — it is built by consistency. Governments invest enormous energy in explaining themselves: press releases, public campaigns, transparency portals. But citizens do not primarily form their views of government through official channels. They form them through repeated, direct encounters — the clerk who helped them or didn't, the permit that came through or was lost in the system, the road that was fixed or ignored. Every interaction is a data point. Communication can never outrun the accumulation of those data points. If the experience is bad, the messaging makes it worse.

Reform fatigue is real, and it is rational. One of the most demoralizing patterns I have watched over three decades is the reform cycle: a new administration arrives, announces sweeping changes to the bureaucracy, reorganizes ministries, rebrands programs, and declares a new era of governance. Then, four or five years later, the next administration arrives and does it again — often dismantling what the previous one built, rebranding what was just rebranded. Citizens learn, correctly, that announced change is not the same as change. Their skepticism is not apathy. It is the outcome of paying attention.

The people closest to the problem usually know the most about it. This sounds obvious. It is, apparently, very hard to act on. The frontline civil servant who processes welfare applications, the nurse navigator in a public hospital, the housing officer who meets evicted families — these people carry operational knowledge that no policy document captures. And yet the design of most reform processes runs in exactly the opposite direction: experts at the top define the problem, draft the solution, and then hand it down to be implemented by people who were never asked. I have watched good policies fail because of this. I have watched mediocre policies succeed because someone, somewhere in the middle of the system, quietly adapted them to fit the actual situation on the ground.

None of this is pessimistic, though I know it can read that way. The point is not that government cannot change — it can, and it has, in ways that genuinely matter. Korea's expansion of public health coverage, its digital services infrastructure, its shift toward more participatory local governance: these are real achievements, built by real people doing difficult work over long periods of time.

The point is that change in government is slower, messier, and more dependent on unglamorous work than the reform industry tends to acknowledge. The breakthroughs rarely happen at the announcement. They happen later, quietly, when someone in the system figures out how to make the idea actually work — and when enough political will exists to let them.

That is what I try to teach. Not cynicism, and not naive optimism. Something more like patient attention: the belief that watching how institutions actually function, over time and up close, is the only way to understand how to make them better.

What Seoul's Digital Government Success Really Tells Us

Korea is often held up as a model of digital government — ranked first by the UN three years running. But I think we sometimes mistake the outcome for the lesson.

The infrastructure matters, yes. But what actually drove the transformation was something harder to export: a public that demanded it, a bureaucracy willing to be measured, and a political will to treat digital access as a right rather than a convenience. Technology was the vehicle, not the destination.

When other countries ask "how did Korea do it?", I want to answer: slowly, imperfectly, and with a lot of public pressure. That's the real story.

The Gap Between Policy and People

I've been thinking lately about the distance between a policy document and the person it's meant to serve. That gap — between legislative intent and lived experience — is where most of public administration actually happens. It's also where it most often fails.

A law can be well-designed and still land badly. Implementation is not a technicality. It's the whole point. And yet it remains the least glamorous part of the work — which is precisely why it gets neglected.

This is what I keep trying to tell my students: the exciting part isn't writing the policy. It's making it real for the person at the counter who doesn't speak the right language, fill out the right form, or know the right office to walk into.
